Raymarching for Creative Art · Lesson 05 · Building scenes

A camera you can orbit

Lesson 04 nailed the camera at the origin staring down −Z. To frame a scene like an artist — move around it, aim it, zoom — you build a proper pinhole camera from four vectors.

Your win this lesson Build a reusable setCamera and drive the ray direction from it — then orbit the camera around your sphere over time.

Four inputs, three basis vectors

A pinhole camera is fully described by where it is (ro), what it looks at (ta), and which way is up (vup, usually (0,1,0)). From those you build an orthonormal basis attached to the camera:

mat3 setCamera(vec3 ro, vec3 ta, vec3 vup) {
    vec3 ww = normalize(ta - ro);          // forward — into the scene
    vec3 uu = normalize(cross(ww, vup));   // right
    vec3 vv = cross(uu, ww);               // up (re-orthogonalized)
    return mat3(uu, vv, ww);               // columns
}

Per pixel, the ray direction is the basis applied to the UV, pushed forward by the focal length (your zoom):

vec3 rd = normalize(cam * vec3(uv, fov));   // fov ~1.0 wide, ~2.5 zoomed-in
Watch for If the look direction is parallel to vup (camera pointing straight up/down), cross(ww, vup) is zero and the basis blows up into NaNs. Pick a different vup for those shots.

Play — orbit and zoom

Drag on the canvas to orbit the camera (horizontal = angle, vertical = height). Let go and it auto-orbits. The fov slider zooms. The whole scene rotates for free — because you're moving the camera, not the objects.

Now you — make it orbit, graded live

The camera is parked. Your task: place ro on a circle around the origin that advances with iTime, so the view rotates. Check verifies the image actually changes over time.
