Normals, light & shadows

A hit point is a silhouette. To make it read as a lit object you need the surface normal — and with an SDF the normal comes for free. Add diffuse, a soft shadow, and a line of fog, and you have a finished raymarched image.

Your win this lesson Compute SDF normals, light a surface with diffuse + ambient, and assemble a complete, good-looking raymarched scene — the thing your whole mission was pointing at.

Normals from the SDF gradient

Why the gradient? The distance field grows fastest as you move straight away from the surface — so the direction of steepest increase is the outward normal. To find it you don't need calculus on your formula: just sample map at a few tiny offsets around the point and see which way distance climbs. IQ's trick samples the four corners of a tiny tetrahedron (the offsets in e; 0.5773 ≈ 1/√3 is just what makes those four directions evenly spread) and combines them into the gradient:

vec3 calcNormal(vec3 p) {
    vec2 e = vec2(1.0, -1.0) * 0.5773 * 0.0005;   // 0.0005 = how far to sample; 0.5773 = 1/√3
    return normalize(
        e.xyy * map(p + e.xyy) + e.yyx * map(p + e.yyx) +
        e.yxy * map(p + e.yxy) + e.xxx * map(p + e.xxx));
}

You'll see this exact four-tap appear in almost every later lesson — it's the standard way to get a normal from any map.

Diffuse + ambient

Lambert's law: brightness is how directly the surface faces the light, max(dot(n, L), 0.0). Add a small ambient term so the shadowed side isn't pure black — without it, scenes look like they're on the Moon:

float diff = max(dot(n, lightDir), 0.0);
vec3 col = ambient * material + diff * material;

Soft shadows & fog — the cheap magic

March a second ray from the hit point toward the light; if it gets blocked, you're in shadow. IQ's soft-shadow keeps the closest approach for a free penumbra. One line of exponential fog then gives instant depth. A final pow(col, 0.4545) gamma pass makes it look right on screen.

Now you — light it, graded live

A single sphere, currently flat gray. Light it: compute the normal and apply diffuse (Task 1), then add ambient so the dark side keeps some color (Task 2). Check grades each by comparing the lit top to the shadowed bottom.

Watch for Compute the normal only at the hit point, never inside the march loop. And nudge the shadow ray off the surface (p + n*0.02) or it instantly shadows itself — "shadow acne."
