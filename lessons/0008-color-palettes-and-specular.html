Color: cosine palettes & specular

Technique gets you a lit sphere. Color is what makes it art. Two tools do most of the work: IQ's cosine palettes for rich, controllable color, and a specular highlight to make surfaces look like they catch light.

Your win this lesson Drive surface color with a cosine palette and add a specular highlight — the two moves that take a render from "diagram" to "image."

Cosine palettes

A whole color ramp from four vec3 constants. t is any 0→1 driver — depth, height, normal, iteration count — and the cosine cycles smoothly through hues:

vec3 palette(float t) {
    vec3 a = vec3(0.5), b = vec3(0.5);       // base + amplitude
    vec3 c = vec3(1.0), d = vec3(0.0,0.33,0.67); // frequency + phase per channel
    return a + b*cos(6.28318*(c*t + d));
}

The phase d offsets red/green/blue differently, so the ramp sweeps through a designed range instead of plain grayscale. Change four numbers, get a new mood.

Why a cosine? Because it's smooth and it loops — no hard edges, and the ends meet, so the palette has no ugly seam. Read it per channel as base + amplitude·cos(…): a sets the middle brightness, b how far it swings, c how many colour cycles across the ramp, and d where red, green and blue each start. Three channels, slightly out of phase, is all a rich gradient is.

Specular — the catch-light

Diffuse is how much a surface faces the light. Specular is a shiny reflection of the light toward the camera — bright where the half-vector between light and view aligns with the normal:

vec3 h = normalize(lightDir - rd);          // half-vector (view dir = -rd)
float spec = pow(max(dot(n, h), 0.0), shininess);  // tight, bright highlight
col += spec * vec3(1.0);                      // add a white glint
Why -rd, and why "half" rd points from the camera into the scene, so the direction back to the viewer is -rd. The half-vector is the average of "toward the light" and "toward the eye": it points exactly where the surface would have to face to bounce the light straight into your eye. So when the normal lines up with h (dot near 1) you're looking right at the reflection — the glint. The pow(…, shininess) just squeezes that into a small bright spot: higher power = tighter, sharper highlight.

Watch for Specular goes on top of diffuse, added not multiplied, and uses white (the light's color), not the material color. High shininess → tiny sharp glint; low → broad sheen.
